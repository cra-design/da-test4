Enter personal information about yourself so the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) can identify you and accurately calculate the benefits and credits you may be entitled to.

Filing your tax return electronically

There are many certified tax software you can choose from to do your taxes, some of which are free, and each is a bit different. When using a software, you will answer some questions at the start of the process about your personal situation. Some software makes suggestions based on the questions you answered, while others do not provide suggestions and require you to enter certain amounts yourself.

Most software guide you through the process so you do not miss out on any deductions, credits or benefits that you may be entitled to. However, it is always a good idea to know what is available to make sure you are not missing out.

If you are using certified tax software, it will do the calculations for you automatically and may not show the individual steps.

Most software advise you of any issues or errors with your tax return so you can correct them before submitting the return to the CRA. Although the software does the calculations for you, you are ultimately responsible for the tax return that you submit, so it is important to understand how the calculations are completed.

Doing your taxes every year

It is very important to do your taxes every year, even if you had no income in the year.

The CRA uses the information from your taxes to determine if you are entitled to a tax refund or any benefit and credit payments, like the goods and services tax / harmonized sales tax (GST/HST) credit or the Canada child benefit.

Other government departments also use information from your tax return to calculate other payments. For example, Service Canada uses the information to calculate the guaranteed income supplement (GIS) for adults 65 or older. Not filing your taxes by the due date will interrupt your GIS payments.

Providing your personal information (part 1 of 8)

Why provide information about yourself

When you fill out Step 1 of your income tax and benefit return, you must provide your personal identification and other information. This is so the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) can identify you and accurately calculate the benefits and credits you may be entitled to.

Income tax and benefit return
Income tax and benefit return

The CRA uses the information you provide in Step 1 of your tax return to communicate with you and to calculate certain benefits. Incorrect or incomplete information can cause delays or inaccurate calculations. It is important to provide the information to the best of your knowledge.

The CRA ensures your information is kept confidential.

Protect yourself

The CRA will not ask for information about your passport, health card, or driver’s licence.

Updating your information with the CRA before you file your taxes electronically

You cannot update certain personal information, such as your address or name, by entering the new information into the certified tax software. Be sure to update this information with the CRA before you use the software.

You may be able to update this information using My Account. By registering for a CRA account, you can access helpful services to complete your tax return.

You can also contact the CRA to make changes to your personal information.

Providing your name, address, and social insurance number

Full name

You must provide your first and last name. If you are using certified tax software, the name you put on your tax return must match what is on file with the CRA.

To change your name, please contact the CRA by phone or by mail.

Example: Reporting your name

Charles Bélanger-Chapleau filed his first tax return using his full name. He must use this same name every year, unless he contacts the CRA to change it. Using only "Charles Bélanger" may create problems in the CRA's system.

Mailing address

You must provide your complete mailing address. You can update your mailing address on My Account or by contacting the CRA.

Social insurance number (SIN)

You must provide your SIN, which is a 9-digit number (for example, 111 111 111) that identifies you for income tax purposes. It also allows you to work in Canada and receive benefits and services from the government.

Your SIN is unique, personal, and confidential. You must provide your SIN to your employer, your financial institution, and certain government departments.

For individuals who do not have a SIN

Provide your individual tax number (ITN) or temporary tax number (TTN) if you received one because you are not eligible for a SIN. Contact the CRA if you need to apply for an ITN or a TTN.

Contact Service Canada if you are eligible for a SIN but do not have one.

Providing your date of birth, language of correspondence, and email address

Date of birth

You must provide your complete date of birth. The CRA uses your date of birth to check your identity.

The CRA must have your complete date of birth on record for you to submit your return electronically. You can contact the CRA to confirm what information is on file before doing your taxes electronically.

Language of correspondence

You must choose either English or French. The CRA communicates with you in the official language of your choice.

Email address

If you have a CRA account and want to receive your CRA correspondence electronically include your email address on your tax return to register for “Electronic mail.” You can also set your correspondence preferences to “Electronic mail” in your My Account. If you choose “Electronic mail”, the CRA will email you to tell you when correspondence is waiting for you in your My Account.

If your correspondence preference is set to “Electronic mail” but you want to continue receiving your correspondence by mail, you can unselect your correspondence preference in My Account.

Tip

If you don’t have access to a CRA account and prefer to receive your correspondence by mail, don’t include your email address on your tax return.

Other questions you may need to answer

Entering or leaving Canada

If you became a resident of Canada in the tax year, you must provide your date of entry. If you left Canada in the tax year and became a resident of another country, you must provide your date of departure.

Residing on the settlement lands of Indigenous governments

In some provinces and territories, you are required to answer questions to identify if you resided on the settlement lands of an Indigenous government on December 31 of the tax year.

These Indigenous governments are:

  • Nunatsiavut Government (in Newfoundland and Labrador)
  • Tłı̨chǫ (Tlicho) Government and Délı̨nę Got’ı̨nę Government (in the Northwest Territories)
  • 11 self-governing Yukon First Nations

Only the tax return for the provinces or territories that are listed above will include questions about residence on settlement lands.

Exempt income

You must let the CRA know whether you have any income that is exempt from tax under section 87 of the Indian Act.

If you check this box, you must fill out Form T90, Income Exempt from Tax under the Indian Act, so the CRA can calculate your Canada workers benefit, if it applies, and your family's provincial or territorial benefits. The CRA will also use the information you provide on Form T90 to calculate your Canada training credit limit.

Note: The CRA uses the term "Indian" because it has a legal meaning in the Indian Act.

Reporting your income (part 1 of 8)

Income explained

Income is amounts you earn or receive during the tax year through salary, social benefits, property, investments or professional activities. You could also receive amounts other than your salary, such as tips or occasional earnings from work you do on the side.

Example: What is considered income

Malika has a summer job as a food delivery driver. The money she earns, including tips, is considered her income.

In Step 2 of your income tax and benefit return, you must report your income from all sources for the tax year. This includes income from all sources inside and outside Canada. The total of these amounts is known as your total income.

Income tax and benefit return

Report

The taxes that you might owe are calculated based on your income. The Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) operates several review programs to make sure that your income is reported correctly. If you do not report all of your income, you may have to pay penalties and interest as well as the tax you should have paid on the missing income.

Fast fact

Tips controlled by your employer will be reported on your tax slip as “employment income.” You need to report cash tips given to you directly as “other employment income.”

Information slips explained

Most income you receive will be reported on an information slip, sometimes called a tax slip. This is a tax document given to you by whoever paid you the income, such as your employer or financial institution. Your income information is reported on the slip in numbered boxes that each refer to a specific line on the tax return. Each information slip has instructions about where to report the amounts on your tax return.

Even though not all your income may be reported on an information slip, such as tips or occasional earnings, you are still responsible for reporting it on your tax return. You should keep track of all the money you earn throughout the year, especially tips that are not recorded on your paystubs, so you are ready at tax time.

Common types of income

Employment income

You’ll receive a T4 slip if you earned or received income from your employer, such as salary, wages, commissions, bonuses, or tips. You will see the amount to report on your tax return in box 14 of your T4 slip.

Other employment income, including direct tips

This amount most commonly includes cash tips paid directly to you because you won’t receive an information slip for them. This amount also includes occasional earnings, such as from babysitting or other odd jobs.

If you are self-employed and earn income through independent work, that income is not reported in this section. For more information on being self-employed, take the module: Working for yourself.

Exempt income

If you are registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act, you may have earned or received tax-exempt employment or self-employment income that will be reported on a T4 slip. You will see the amounts in boxes 71 or 88. You should not report these amounts on your tax return. Instead, report them on Form T90, Income Exempt from Tax under the Indian Act. The CRA will use the information you give on Form T90 to calculate certain benefits and credits.

Note: The Canada Revenue Agency uses the term “Indian” because it has a legal meaning in the Indian Act.

For more information, see “Tax-exempt income” in part 6 of 8 of this lesson.

You are responsible for reporting all income earned or received, including in cash, even if you did not receive an information slip.

Employment insurance and other benefits

If you received benefits from Service Canada, such as EI or tuition assistance, you’ll receive a T4E slip. You will see the amount to report on your tax return in box 14.

Interest and other investment income

If you received this type of income from your bank or financial institution, you’ll usually receive a T5 slip, Statement of Investment Income. It uses various box numbers to report income.

You may not receive a T5 slip if the amount is less than $50, but you must still report the income. You should check your financial statements to confirm if you received this type of income during the year.

Scholarships, fellowships, bursaries, and artists' project grants

If you received any of these amounts during the year, you will receive a T4A slip. The full payments you received are shown in box 105. However, you may not need to report the entire amount on your tax return, depending on the type of scholarship received, what kind of education program you are enrolled in, and whether you are in school full-time or part-time.

Fast fact

If you are a part-time student who is eligible for the disability tax credit you may not need to report your scholarship on your tax return.
